'''Kevin John Bowyer''' (born 9 January 1961 in [[Southend-on-Sea]]) is an [[England|English]] [[pipe organ|organist]], known for his prolific [[sound recording|recording]] and [[recital]] career and his performances of unusual, [[contemporary classical music|modern]] and extremely difficult compositions.

==Biography==

He sang in a [[choir]] and learnt the [[piano accordion]] and [[pipe organ|organ]] as a child. When the [[church (building)|church]] where he practised refused to let him carry on practising, he says: "I went and had a key cut to the church and I got in anyway."<ref name=mus>[http://www.musicteachers.co.uk/journal/index.php?issue=2000-07&file=bowyer&page=1 Musicteachers.com] online journal, Volume 2 Issue 1, July 2000</ref> At St. Mary's Church, [[Twickenham]], where he was organist, he managed to ''confuse the congregation'' in a way similar to that of [[Bach]] by playing [[Malcolm Williamson]]'s ''Vision of Christ-Phœnix'' to conclude a [[confirmation]]: "the ''outcry'' there was at the AGM and in the parish magazine!"<ref>Notes to NI 5509: Organ Music by Peter Maxwell Davies, Jonathan Harvey & Malcolm Williamson</ref> He attended Cecil Jones High School in Southend, and studied at the [[Royal Academy of Music]] from 1979 to 1982 with organists [[Christopher Bowers-Broadbent]] and Douglas Hawkridge, [[harpsichord]]ist Virginia Black, and [[Paul Steinitz]]. After graduation, he studied for two years with [[David Sanger (organist)|David Sanger]] after winning a Countess of [[Munster]] scholarship. When given a list of music to prepare at his first meeting with Sanger, he didn't realise that it was a term's work and had learnt it all by the next week.<ref name=mus/>

Aside from playing the organ, he reads [[modernist literature|modern literature]], especially [[James Joyce]], [[Samuel Beckett]] and the [[Powys (surname)|Powys family]]. He is a lover of [[Cask ale#Real ale|real ale]] and [[malt whisky]], and lists his favourite recreation as "[[sleep]]ing". He also enjoys the odd pinch of [[snuff]].<ref name=kev/>

==Repertoire and performances==

While a student, he performed the complete [[organ symphony|organ symphonies]] of [[Charles-Marie Widor]], [[Louis Vierne]] and [[Marcel Dupré]] (none of which he has yet recorded), and the complete organ music of [[Olivier Messiaen]]. He was able to do this because, he says, "When I was 21, I developed a technique that allowed me to learn a French organ symphony every month" and "always started at the end and then worked backwards."<ref name=mus/> His debut recital was at the [[Royal Festival Hall]] in 1984.<ref name=grove>Ian Carson: ''Bowyer, Kevin (John)'', Grove Music Online ed. L. Macy (Accessed 2007-06-24), http://www.grovemusic.com/</ref>

He has won the following competitions, though he says "the standard hasn't been all that impressive":<ref name=mus/><ref name=grove/>
* [[St Albans International Organ Festival]] 1983 (neither 2nd nor 3rd prize was awarded that year)<ref name=mus/>
* [[Odense]] International Organ Competition 1990
* [[Paisley]] International Organ Festival 1990
* [[Dublin]] International Organ and Choral Festival 1990
* [[Calgary]] International Organ Festival 1990

He has performed and broadcasted all over the world, and has released around ninety recordings, including all of [[Johann Sebastian Bach|Bach]]'s organ music for the Nimbus recording label. His [[recital]] [[organ repertoire|repertoire]] is enormous and ever expanding; in an article restricted to [[Europe]]an organ music of the [[20th century classical music|twentieth century]], he mentions over one hundred [[composer]]s whose music he has played.<ref name=kev>Kevin Bowyer: ''20th Century European Organ Music: A Toast'', in ''The [[Incorporated Association of Organists|IAO]] Millennium Book'', ed. P. Hale, ISBN 0-9538711-0-X</ref> Though he sees contemporary music as his ''[[vocation]]'', he plays organ music from the [[Renaissance music|Renaissance]] and [[Baroque music|Baroque]] periods onwards, and has shown an appreciation for the qualities of [[historical instruments]] in such music.<ref>"I try to take primary sources and playing practices as a starting point, and try to apply them to the instrument that I am using. I am perfectly happy to admit that I am not at the forefront of Bach scholarship because I am a player. Although I rely on the editors of good Bach editions, you have to be aware of all the sources, see for yourself what the differences are and make decisions accordingly.[http://www.musicteachers.co.uk/journal/index.php?issue=2000-07&file=bowyer&page=4 Interview with Kevin Bowyer from MusicTeachers.co.uk]</ref><ref>Kevin Bowyer: Notes to ''A Late 20th Century Edwardian Bach Recital''</ref>

He is the only person to have played and recorded [[Kaikhosru Shapurji Sorabji|Kaikhosru Sorabji]]'s First Organ Symphony in its entirety.<ref>[http://www.sorabji-archive.co.uk/compositions/piece.php?pieceid=39 KSS39 Symphony for Organ] - full history of this work at the [[Sorabji Archive]]</ref>

{{cquote2|That it [Sorabji's First Organ Symphony] is finally becoming known, more than sixty years later [than it was published, in 1925], is due entirely to the uniquely enterprising spirit, astounding prowess and unending and unbending patience and dedication of the most outstanding organist of his generation, who, in his continuing work in preparing for performance and recording Sorabji's Second and Third Organ Symphonies (which of necessity includes fair-copying them longhand), is singlehandedly rewriting the history of organ music since [[Liszt]].|Alistair Hinton, 1988<ref>Notes to Bowyer's recording of Sorabji's Organ Symphony No. 1, [[Continuum Records]] CCD 1001/2</ref>}}

He was organist of the Parish of [[Warwick]] from 1989 until 1998; during this time, he taught around the country for the St Giles International Organ School.<ref name=kev/> In 2005 he was appointed university organist at the [[University of Glasgow]] (with access to the [[Harrison & Harrison]]/[[Henry Willis & Sons|Willis]] organ in the [[University of Glasgow Memorial Chapel|University Memorial Chapel]]), while continuing his teaching career at the [[Royal Northern College of Music]] in [[Manchester]] and the [[Royal Scottish Academy of Music and Drama]] in [[Glasgow]]. New projects include the annual ''Glasgow International Organ Festival''<ref>[http://www.giof.org.uk Glasgow International Organ Festival]</ref> and ''Glasgow Pipeworks''<ref>[http://www.glasgowpipeworks.com Glasgow Pipeworks, Home]</ref> series of recitals of [[contemporary classical music|new music]] for organ.

On learning music, he says: "I practise bits and pieces of it over and over again until my [[finger]]s are moving faster than my [[brain]], then I home in on what is difficult and link these with the easier passages, but the easier passages are still no less learned than the difficult ones. Sometimes it's necessary to practise for twelve to fourteen hours a day, during which you need to keep your mind alert."<ref name=mus/> A particular example has been when he had to learn [[Niccolo Castiglioni]]'s ''Sinfonia Guerriere et Amorose'', 41 minutes of "nearly unplayable music. [...] I set my mind to encompass it in an eight-day learning period, a frame-work the piece naturally slipped into."<ref name=kev/>

Since 2008 he has been able, with the support of the Glasgow University Trust, to be engaged almost exclusively<ref>"Since November 2008 I have put well over 2,000 hours of preparation and rehearsal into the piece. My practice schedule usually began at 6.30 am and ran through, with a couple of breaks for coffee and one for lunch, until 9 pm. Twelve and thirteen hour working days were the norm. Yet even with this gruelling schedule the ramparts of Organ Symphony 2 are only just beginning to crack." - Kevin Bowyer</ref> in preparing for performances of Sorabji's three organ symphonies, the difficulty of which are considerable:

{{cquote2|In my career as a player of contemporary organ music I've played most of the notorious, "super-difficult" pieces — [[Iannis Xenakis]]' Gmeeoorh, [[Brian Ferneyhough]]'s Sieben Sterne, [[Niccolò Castiglioni]]'s Sinfonie Guerriere et Amorose, etc. Sorabji's First Organ Symphony makes all those pieces look like [[Associated Board of the Royal Schools of Music#Graded Exams|grade 2]] finger exercises. And Organ Symphony 2 makes Organ Symphony 1 look like — a grade 2 finger exercise.|Kevin Bowyer<ref name="OS2">http://www.sorabji-archive.co.uk/forum/viewtopic.php?f=10&t=233#p1712</ref>}}

The lengths are also considerable: the Second Symphony alone is over an hour longer than Messiaen's complete organ music put together.<ref name="OS2"/> The Second Symphony was premiered in 2010 and there were several postponements due to the incredible difficulty of learning it.<ref>http://www.sorabji-archive.co.uk/forum/viewtopic.php?f=6&t=42</ref><ref name="OS2"/> The Third Organ Symphony is expected to be premiered in 2014 in [[Glasgow]]. Recordings of the three works are to be made, but not by [[Altarus Records]] as originally planned. Bowyer is also preparing new editions of the scores for publication.<ref>http://www.sorabji-organ.org/aims.html</ref>
