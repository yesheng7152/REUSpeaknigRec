'''Calypso''' ({{pronEng|kəˈlɪpsoʊ}} {{respell|kə|LIP|soh}}, or as in Greek ''Καλυψώ)'' is a [[natural satellite|moon]] of [[Saturn]]. It was discovered by [[Dan Pascu|Pascu]], [[P. Kenneth Seidelmann|Seidelmann]], [[William A. Baum|Baum]] and [[Douglas G. Currie|Currie]] in 1980 from ground-based observations, and was provisionally designated '''{{nowrap|S/1980 S 25}}'''.<ref>[http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03400/03496.html IAUC 3496: ''Satellites of Saturn''] July 31, 1980 (discovery)</ref> Several other apparitions of it were recorded in the following months: {{nowrap|S/1980 S 29}}, {{nowrap|S/1980 S 30}},<ref>[http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03500/03549.html IAUC 3549: ''Satellites of Saturn''] December 11, 1980</ref> {{nowrap|S/1980 S 32}},<ref>[http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03600/03605.html IAUC 3605: ''Satellites of Saturn''] May 18, 1981</ref> and {{nowrap|S/1981 S 2}}.<ref>[http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03500/03593.html IAUC 3593: ''Satellites of Saturn''] April 16, 1981</ref>

In 1983 it was officially named after [[Calypso (mythology)|Calypso]] of [[Greek mythology]]<ref>Transactions of the International Astronomical Union, Vol. XVIIIA, 1982 (confirms Janus, names Epimetheus, Telesto, Calypso) <small>(mentioned in [http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03800/03872.html IAUC 3872: ''Satellites of Jupiter and Saturn''], September 30, 1983)</small></ref>. It is also designated as '''{{nowrap|Saturn XIV}}''' or '''Tethys C'''.

Calypso is co-orbital with the moon [[Tethys (moon)|Tethys]], and resides in Tethys' trailing [[Lagrangian point]] ({{L5}}) 60 degrees behind Tethys. This relationship was first identified by Seidelmann ''et al.'' in 1981.<ref name="Seidelmann1981">Seidelmann, P. K.; [[Robert S. Harrington|Harrington, R. S.]]; Pascu, D.; Baum, W. A.; Currie, D. G.; [[James A. Westphal|Westphal, J. A.]]; and [[G. Edward Danielson|Danielson, G. E.]]; [http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1981Icar...47..282S&amp;db_key=AST&amp;data_type=HTML&amp;format=&amp;high=45eb6e10af20904 ''Saturn Satellite Observations and Orbits from the 1980 Ring Plane Crossing''], Icarus, Vol. 47 (August 1981), pp. 282–287</ref> The moon [[Telesto (moon)|Telesto]] also resides in the other (leading) lagrangian point of Tethys, 60 degrees in the other direction from Tethys. 

[[Image:Calypso moon.jpg|thumb|left|150px|Calypso as seen by [[Voyager 2]]]]
Like many other small Saturnian moons and small asteroids, Calypso is irregularly shaped, has overlapping large craters, and appears to also have loose surface material capable of smoothing the craters' appearance. Its surface is one of the most reflective (at visual wavelengths) in the solar system, with a visual [[geometric albedo]] of 1.34.<ref name=Verbiscer/>  This very high albedo is the result of the sandblasting of particles from Saturn's E-ring, a faint ring composed of small, water-ice particle generated by Enceladus' south polar geysers.

Not to be confused with [[asteroid]] [[53 Kalypso]].
