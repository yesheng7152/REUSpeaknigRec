'''Telesto''' ({{pronEng|tɨˈlɛstoʊ}} {{respell|tə|LES|toe}}, or as Greek ''Τελεστώ)'' is a [[natural satellite|moon]] of [[Saturn]]. It was discovered by [[Bradford A. Smith|Smith]], [[Harold Reitsema|Reitsema]], [[Stephen M. Larson|Larson]] and [[John W. Fountain|Fountain]] in 1980 from ground-based observations, and was provisionally designated '''{{nowrap|S/1980 S 13}}'''.<ref>[http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03400/03466.html IAUC 3466: ''Satellites of Saturn''] 1980 April 10 (discovery)</ref> In the following months, several other apparitions were observed: {{nowrap|S/1980 S 24}}<ref>[http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03400/03484.html IAUC 3484: ''Satellites of Saturn''] 1980 June 6,</ref> {{nowrap|S/1980 S 33}},<ref>[http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03600/03605.html IAUC 3605: ''Satellites of Saturn''] 1981 May 18</ref> and {{nowrap|S/1981 S 1}}.<ref>[http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03500/03593.html IAUC 3593: ''Satellites of Saturn''] 1981 April 16</ref>

In 1983 it was officially named after [[Telesto (mythology)|Telesto]] of [[Greek mythology]].<ref>Transactions of the International Astronomical Union, Vol. XVIIIA, 1982 (confirms Janus, names Epimetheus, Telesto, Calypso) <small>(mentioned in [http://cfa-www.harvard.edu/iauc/03800/03872.html IAUC 3872: ''Satellites of Jupiter and Saturn''], 1983 September 30)</small></ref> It is also designated as '''{{nowrap|Saturn XIII}}''' or '''Tethys B'''.

Telesto is co-orbital with [[Tethys (moon)|Tethys]], residing in Tethys' leading [[Lagrangian point]] ({{L4}}). This relationship was first identified by Seidelmann ''et al.''<ref name="Seidelmann1981">[[P. Kenneth Seidelmann|Seidelmann, P. K.]]; [[Robert S. Harrington|Harrington, R. S.]]; [[Dan Pascu|Pascu, D.]]; [[William A. Baum|Baum, W. A.]]; [[Douglas G. Currie|Currie, D. G.]]; [[James A. Westphal|Westphal, J. A.]]; and [[G. Edward Danielson|Danielson, G. E.]]; [http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1981Icar...47..282S&amp;db_key=AST&amp;data_type=HTML&amp;format=&amp;high=45eb6e10af20904 ''Saturn Satellite Observations and Orbits from the 1980 Ring Plane Crossing''], Icarus, Vol. 47 (August 1981), pp. 282–287</ref> The moon [[Calypso (moon)|Calypso]] also resides in the other (trailing) lagrangian point of Tethys, 60 degrees in the other direction from Tethys. 

The ''[[Cassini-Huygens Mission|Cassini]]'' probe performed a distant flyby of Telesto on October 11, 2005. The resulting images show that its surface is surprisingly smooth, devoid of small [[impact crater]]s.
